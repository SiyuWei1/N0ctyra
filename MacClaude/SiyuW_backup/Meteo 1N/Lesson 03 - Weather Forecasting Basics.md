# Lesson 3: Weather Forecasting Basics - From Past to Present

## Sections Overview

### 1. A Craving for Weather Knowledge
- **Benjamin Franklin**: speculated about moist heated air, precipitation, global circulation; called for widespread weather observations
  - 1743 lunar eclipse: deduced that Nor'Easters originate in the south and travel northeast
- **Thomas Jefferson & James Madison**: recorded that coldest time = sunrise, warmest = 3-4 PM; higher elevations = cooler
- **Joseph Henry** (first Smithsonian director): started nationwide weather mapping in 1849; 150+ volunteers reporting by telegraph → 600 by 1860
- **1870**: Congress created a national weather bureau
- Early success measured by Great Lakes shipping losses: budget cuts in 1882-83 → shipping losses doubled ($1.5M → $3M), proving forecasts' value

### 2. Folklore
- Weather folklore = stories passed down correlating weather with unrelated things
- Examples: woolly bear caterpillars, squirrels gathering nuts, cows lying down, aching joints
- Most folklore has **no scientific basis**; some have partial truth
- Important: folklore should **never** replace official forecasts

### 3. Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP)
- **Lewis Fry Richardson** (early 1900s): first attempted numerical weather forecasting by hand; took months to compute a 6-hour forecast
- **ENIAC** (1950): first electronic computer used for weather forecasting
- Modern **supercomputers** (NOAA's Hera): perform **8+ quadrillion calculations/second**
- NWP uses **mathematical equations** based on physics to predict future atmospheric state
- Models divide atmosphere into **3D grid boxes**; smaller grid = more detail but more computing power needed

### 4. Types of Forecast Models
- **Global models**: cover entire Earth, coarser resolution (e.g., GFS — American model, ECMWF — European model)
- **Regional/mesoscale models**: cover smaller areas with finer detail (e.g., NAM, HRRR)
- **Ensemble forecasting**: running the same model many times with slightly different initial conditions → shows range of possible outcomes and uncertainty
- **Spaghetti plots**: visual display of ensemble members; tighter clustering = higher confidence

### 5. Forecast Accuracy and Time Ranges
- **Short-range** (0-3 days): most accurate
- **Medium-range** (4-7 days): reasonably accurate for general trends
- **Extended-range** (8-14 days): broad trends only, low detail accuracy
- **Beyond 14 days**: very limited skill; seasonal outlooks give general tendencies
- Modern 5-day forecasts are as accurate as **3-day forecasts were 20 years ago**
- Forecasts have improved dramatically, but will **never be perfect** due to chaos in the atmosphere

### 6. The Human Element
- Despite computer models, **human forecasters** still add value
- Meteorologists interpret model data, recognize model biases, communicate risks
- Communication of forecasts is just as important as the forecast itself

## 中文总结

**第3课：天气预报基础——从过去到现在**

本课讲述了天气预报从民间传说到超级计算机的发展历程。

**历史演变：**
- 本杰明·富兰克林最早呼吁建立广泛的气象观测网络
- 约瑟夫·亨利在1849年通过电报建立了全国性观测网络
- 1870年美国国会创建了国家气象局
- 早期通过五大湖航运损失证明了天气预报的经济价值

**民间传说 vs 科学预报：**
- 很多天气谚语（如"关节疼痛预示坏天气"）缺乏科学依据，绝不应替代官方预报

**数值天气预报（NWP）：**
- 用物理方程和数学模型预测大气未来状态
- 现代超级计算机每秒可执行8千万亿次运算
- **全球模型**（GFS、ECMWF）覆盖全球但分辨率较粗；**区域模型**（NAM、HRRR）覆盖较小区域但更精细
- **集合预报**：多次运行模型取不同初始条件，显示预报不确定性

**预报准确度：**
- 短期（0-3天）最准确；中期（4-7天）大趋势可靠；超过14天技能很有限
- 现在的5天预报和20年前的3天预报一样准确
- 由于大气的混沌性质，预报永远不可能100%完美

**核心要点**：人类预报员仍然不可替代——他们解读模型数据、识别模型偏差、有效沟通风险信息。
