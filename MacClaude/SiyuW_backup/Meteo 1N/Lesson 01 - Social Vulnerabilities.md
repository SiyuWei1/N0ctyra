# Lesson 1: Social Vulnerabilities

## Sections Overview

### 1. Defining Risk: The Fault Tree
- **Risk** = Probability of event occurring × Magnitude of event's effects
- **Fault Tree Analysis**: diagram illustrating all failure points; uses **OR gates** (any input triggers hazard) and **AND gates** (all inputs required)
- Risk assessors practice **conservativeness** — consciously over-estimate risk
- No two weather events are identical; never compare storms

### 2. A Better Way to Define Risk: Swiss Cheese
- **R = H / O** (Risk = Hazard / Outrage)
  - Hazard = what risk assessors care about (probability, severity)
  - Outrage = what the public cares about (personal concern)
  - R = 1 → **risk assessment harmony** (balanced)
  - R > 1 → high hazard, low outrage (people don't care enough)
  - R < 1 → low hazard, high outrage (people overreact)
- **Swiss Cheese Model**: multiple lines of defense between hazard and disaster
  - **Active failures**: bad human decisions (intentional or unintentional)
  - **Latent failures**: hidden problems in equipment/infrastructure
  - Disaster occurs when all "holes" align

### 3. Outrage is the Engine; Hazard is the Car
- Correlation between actual danger and public concern is only **~0.2** (very low)
- Top 3 weather killers (average): **extreme heat, floods, rip currents**
- Bottom killers: hurricanes, lightning, winter weather (despite high public fear)
- **"Outrage is the engine of hazard perception"** — people's feelings drive how they perceive threats
- **Hazard advocacy**: when danger is high but people don't care → convince them to act
- **Outrage suppression**: when people panic over low threat → calm them down

### 4. Vulnerability and a Paradigm Shift
- **Vulnerability**: the state of being exposed to potential physical or emotional harm
- **Dominant Disaster Paradigm**: blames nature ("Acts of God"), focuses on physical processes
- **Vulnerability Paradigm**: emphasizes social/economic/political factors; disasters have both natural and social components; social component is the main driver

### 5. Class (Part 1 & Part 2)
- **Class**: social grouping based on income, wealth, occupation, education
- **Social stratification**: uneven distribution creating layers in society
- 3-tier model: Upper class (≥$300K/yr), Middle class ($37.5K–$300K), Lower class (≤$37.5K)
- Top 20% holds **52%** of total income; top 1% holds **33%** of total wealth
- **Poverty line** (2023): $18,310 for household of 2; $27,750 for family of 4; 12%+ Americans below it
- **Wealth**: total financial value after debts; top 1% > bottom 90% combined
- **Social capital**: ability to obtain resources/favors from personal connections
- Upward class mobility is extremely difficult; most stay in birth class

### 6. Gender and Disaster
- Women earn **16% less** than men on average
- **Single Female-Headed Households (SFHH)**: lower incomes, less education, sub-standard housing
- 40% of SFHH cannot recover within a year vs. 15% of SMHH
- Men die more in every weather disaster type — men tolerate more risk, respond to warnings with skepticism
- Women are more likely to receive and act on warnings; men tend to "stand their ground"

### 7. Age: Not Just a Number
- **Children** (≤18): Pre-disaster risk = not being informed/warned; Post-disaster risk = **PTSD** (~50% show symptoms)
- **Elderly** (≥65): Pre-disaster risk = not being informed; Post-disaster risk = **premature death**
- Elderly population: ~62 million (18% of US), projected to reach 84 million (23%)
- 90% of elderly live at home; increasing isolation with age

### 8. Health and Disability
- ~13% of US population has at least one disability
- ~90% of people 65+ have at least one chronic health issue
- 3 key problems: individual reduced capability, healthcare system inequity, healthcare infrastructure vulnerability
- ~10% of Americans uninsured (30+ million); people of color = <50% of population but >65% of uninsured
- Rural residents: average 17-min drive to hospital vs. 10-min in urban areas

### 9. All in the Family
- **Pets**: ~75% of US households have pets; complicate evacuation significantly
- Pet owners often refuse to evacuate without pets → stay in danger
- **Violence**: disasters intensify existing domestic violence and can create new violence
- Disasters impact families in 4 ways: socially, physically, emotionally, economically

## 中文总结

**第1课：社会脆弱性**

本课是 Meteo 1N 的开篇，核心主题是：**自然灾害本身不造成灾难，社会不平等才是灾难的放大器。**

**关键概念：**
- **风险 = 危害 / 愤怒（R=H/O）**：真正的风险取决于实际危险程度和公众关注程度的平衡。当两者失衡时，要么人们忽视了真正的威胁，要么对不存在的威胁过度恐慌。
- **瑞士奶酪模型**：每一层防御都可能有漏洞（人为失误或隐藏缺陷），只要漏洞不全部对齐，灾难就可以避免。
- **脆弱性范式转变**：过去把灾害全归咎于自然（"天灾"），现在认识到社会因素（阶级、性别、年龄、健康）才是主要驱动力。

**社会脆弱性的五大维度：**
1. **阶级**：收入和财富分配极不均等，贫困家庭缺乏准备和恢复资源
2. **性别**：女性收入低、单亲母亲家庭最脆弱；男性因冒险心理死亡率更高
3. **年龄**：儿童依赖成人保护，灾后易患PTSD；老人信息获取困难，灾后早亡风险高
4. **健康与残疾**：慢性病患者和残障人士在疏散和恢复中面临更大困难
5. **家庭动态**：宠物使疏散复杂化，灾害加剧家庭暴力

**实际意义**：了解自己的社会脆弱性是防灾准备的第一步。每个人都有至少一个脆弱性。
