# Lesson 5: Tornadoes

## Sections Overview

### 1. Getting Spun Up on Tornadoes
- **Tornado**: rapidly rotating column of air in contact with both the ground and base of a cloud
- **Funnel cloud**: rotating column NOT touching the ground (not a tornado yet)
- **Waterspout**: tornado over water; can move onshore

### 2. How Tornadoes Form
- Supercells are the primary producers of tornadoes
- **Vertical wind shear** in the lower atmosphere creates horizontal spinning "noodles" of air
- Supercell updraft tilts the horizontal rotation vertical → forms **mesocyclone**
- Mesocyclone stretches vertically → spins faster (like figure skater pulling arms in = **conservation of angular momentum**)
- Not all mesocyclones produce tornadoes

### 3. Types of Tornadoes
- **Wedge tornado**: wider than tall, often the most powerful
- **Cone tornado**: classic funnel shape, wider at cloud base, narrower at ground
- **Stovepipe tornado**: roughly same width top to bottom
- **Rope tornado**: thin, rope-like; often the beginning or end stage
- **Rain-wrapped tornadoes**: hidden in heavy rain, extremely dangerous because invisible to the eye

### 4. The Enhanced Fujita (EF) Scale
- Rates tornado intensity based on **damage** (not wind speed directly)
- **EF0**: 65-85 mph — light damage
- **EF1**: 86-110 mph — moderate damage
- **EF2**: 111-135 mph — significant damage
- **EF3**: 136-165 mph — severe damage
- **EF4**: 166-200 mph — devastating damage
- **EF5**: >200 mph — incredible destruction
- Ratings assigned AFTER the tornado through damage surveys

### 5. Tornado Climatology
- **Tornado Alley**: Great Plains (Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas, Nebraska, South Dakota) — most frequent tornadoes
- **Dixie Alley**: Southeast US (Mississippi, Alabama, Tennessee) — tornadoes are deadlier here due to terrain, population density, mobile homes, nighttime occurrence
- US averages ~1,200 tornadoes per year
- Peak season varies: spring in the South, late spring/summer in the Plains, summer in the North
- Tornadoes can happen **any time of year, any time of day**, but most common in late afternoon/evening

### 6. Tornado Watches and Warnings
- **Tornado Watch** (SPC): conditions favorable for tornadoes; be prepared; covers large area for several hours
  - **PDS (Particularly Dangerous Situation)** watch: heightened wording for potential strong/violent tornadoes
- **Tornado Warning** (local NWS): tornado indicated by radar or spotted; take shelter IMMEDIATELY
  - Average lead time: **~13 minutes** (but varies)
  - **Tornado Emergency**: highest-level wording within a warning; confirmed violent tornado heading toward populated area

### 7. Safety Tips
- **Best shelter**: basement or interior room on lowest floor (bathroom, closet), away from windows
- **Cover your head** with pillows, blankets, or mattress
- **Mobile homes are NEVER safe** — get out and go to a sturdy building or storm shelter
- If driving: do NOT try to outrun; get to a sturdy building; do NOT shelter under overpasses
- **Know your risk before storm season**; have a plan; practice drills

### 8. Three Points of Structural Failure
1. **Policy failure**: building codes not set high enough
2. **Design failure**: structure not up to code
3. **Construction failure**: not built properly to specifications

### 9. Mobile/Manufactured Homes
- Only ~6% of US housing stock but account for disproportionate share of fatalities
- Residents are **10x more likely to die** in a tornado than permanent home residents
- Per 100 damaged permanent homes: ~1.6 fatalities; per 100 damaged manufactured homes: ~**14 fatalities**
- Unanchored manufactured home can be destroyed by EF1; properly anchored can withstand most EF2

### 10. Tornado Alley vs. Dixie Alley
- **Tornado Alley** (TX, OK, KS, NE): highest frequency
- **Dixie Alley** (LA, AR, MS, AL, TN, GA): more vulnerable — rising tornado frequency + widespread poverty (all 6 states in top 12 poorest) + high density of manufactured homes (>40% in many counties)
- For every **1% increase** in a state's poverty rate, tornado fatalities rise ~**5%**

### 11. Home Improvements for Safety
- Safe rooms / storm shelters can be built for ~$3,000–$10,000
- Garage doors are weakest structural point — reinforcement helps
- **Cost is a major barrier** for low-income families

## 中文总结

**第5课：龙卷风**

本课全面介绍龙卷风的形成、分类、分布和安全措施。

**龙卷风的形成：**
- 超级单体雷暴的低层风切变产生水平旋转空气 → 被上升气流倾斜为垂直旋转 → 形成中气旋 → 进一步拉伸加速 → 龙卷风
- 并非所有中气旋都会产生龙卷风

**四种常见形态：**
楔形（最宽最强）、锥形（经典漏斗）、烟囱形（上下等宽）、绳状（最细，常见于生命周期始末）
- **雨裹龙卷风**：被暴雨包围看不见，极其危险

**增强藤田等级（EF Scale）：** EF0（轻微）→ EF5（毁灭性），根据灾后损害评估评定

**龙卷风地理分布：**
- **龙卷风走廊（Tornado Alley）**：大平原地区，频率最高
- **迪克西走廊（Dixie Alley）**：美国东南部，龙卷风更致命（地形遮挡、夜间发生、移动房屋多）
- 美国年均约1200个龙卷风

**Watch vs Warning vs Emergency：**
- Watch = 条件有利，做准备
- Warning = 雷达探测到或已目击，立即躲避（平均提前约13分钟）
- Tornado Emergency = 最高级别，确认有强龙卷风袭击人口密集区

**安全要点：**
- 去地下室或最低层内部房间，保护头部
- **移动房屋绝不安全**——必须离开
- 开车时不要试图超越龙卷风，不要躲在立交桥下
