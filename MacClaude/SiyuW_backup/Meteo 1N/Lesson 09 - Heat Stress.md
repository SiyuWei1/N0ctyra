# Lesson 9: Physical Stress from High Temperature and Moisture

## Sections Overview

### 1. Heat Climatology
- **90°F** is a reasonable threshold for "hot" in most US climates
- **Excessive heat season**: window of time when 90°F+ has historically been observed
  - Example: Minneapolis — April 15 to October 10 (based on record highs)
  - Can always expand earlier/later, especially with climate change
- **July** is the hottest month for an overwhelming majority of the US and Canada
  - Lag behind summer solstice (June) due to energy budget
- Exception: parts of southern/western US peak in other months (monsoon season in Desert Southwest → August; delayed sea-breeze effects in coastal areas)
- Don't let a cool start to summer lower your guard — heat can appear suddenly

### 2. Humidity Measures
- **Specific humidity**: actual mass of water vapor in air (g/kg); does not change with temperature
- **Relative humidity**: ratio of current moisture to max possible at that temperature (%); changes with temperature
- **Dew point**: best measure of how "muggy" it feels — does not fluctuate with temperature like RH does

### 3. Heat Waves
- **Heat wave**: period of abnormally high temperatures lasting 2+ days (no universal threshold; relative to local climate)
- Duration matters: multi-day heat is far more dangerous than a single hot day
  - Nighttime temperatures staying high = body can't recover → cumulative heat stress
- **Urban Heat Island (UHI)**: urban areas 1-7°F warmer during day, up to 5°F warmer at night
- 1995 Chicago heat wave: **739 deaths** in 5 days — disproportionately elderly, low-income, socially isolated
- 2003 European heat wave: ~**70,000 deaths**
- 2021 Pacific Northwest heat dome: Portland hit **116°F**; Lytton, BC hit **121°F** (Canadian record)

### 4. Heat Index and WBGT
- **Heat Index**: combines air temperature + relative humidity to determine "feels like" temperature (created by Robert Steadman, 1979)
  - Assumes shady, light-wind conditions → can underestimate danger in direct sun
- **Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT)**: more comprehensive — accounts for temperature, humidity, wind speed, sun angle, cloud cover
  - WBGT = 0.7 × Wet Bulb + 0.2 × Globe Temp + 0.1 × Dry Bulb
  - Invented in 1950s by US military
  - 85°F WBGT = elevated risk; 90°F+ = outdoor activities should be prohibited
- **Excessive Heat Watch/Warning/Advisory**:
  - Advisory: heat index 100-104°F for 2+ hours
  - Warning: heat index ≥ 105°F for 2+ hours
  - These thresholds vary by region and acclimation

### 5. Heat-Related Illnesses
- **Heat cramps**: muscle cramps from dehydration/electrolyte loss; mildest form
- **Heat exhaustion**: heavy sweating, weakness, nausea, dizziness, cool/clammy skin; body temp up to ~104°F
  - Move to cool area, hydrate, apply cool cloths
- **Heat stroke**: body temp ≥ 104°F, hot/red/dry skin (sweating may stop), confusion, loss of consciousness
  - **MEDICAL EMERGENCY** — call 911 immediately; can be fatal
  - Cool the person rapidly while waiting for help

### 6. Acclimation
- Takes **1-2 weeks** of exposure to become acclimated to heat
- Unacclimated people are at much higher risk
- Early-season heat waves are most dangerous because bodies aren't acclimated yet
- Traveling from cool to hot climate → temporarily unacclimated
- 2015 Penn State example: 0 days of 90°F all summer → sudden mid-September heat → dozens hospitalized

### 7. Who is Most Vulnerable?
- Elderly (especially those living alone, without AC)
- Young children
- Outdoor workers (construction, agriculture, athletics)
- People with chronic illnesses
- People without air conditioning or access to cooling centers
- People taking certain medications that impair thermoregulation

### 8. Safety Tips
- **Stay hydrated** — drink water before you feel thirsty
- Limit outdoor activity during peak heat (10 AM - 6 PM)
- Wear lightweight, light-colored, loose-fitting clothing
- **Never leave children or pets in a vehicle** — interior temps can exceed 130°F in minutes
- Check on elderly neighbors/relatives
- Know the signs of heat-related illness

## 中文总结

**第9课：高温和湿度带来的身体压力**

高温是美国年均天气致死率最高的灾害类型（30年平均~170人/年，10年平均~204人/年），但常被低估。

**热气候学：**
- 90°F（32°C）是大多数地区"热"的合理门槛
- "极端高温季节"可通过查看历史记录中90°F+出现的时间窗口来确定
- 7月是美国绝大多数地区最热的月份（因为存在太阳辐射滞后效应）
- 不要因为前期凉爽就掉以轻心——突然高温最危险

**热浪与体感温度：**
- 热浪持续多天更危险，尤其是夜间气温不降时身体无法恢复
- **热指数（Heat Index）**：结合气温和相对湿度，反映体感温度
- 城市热岛效应使城市更热

**三种热相关疾病（严重程度递增）：**
1. **热痉挛**：肌肉抽搐，最轻微
2. **热衰竭**：大量出汗、虚弱、恶心、皮肤湿冷，体温升至约104°F
3. **热射病（中暑）**：体温≥104°F、皮肤发红发干（可能停止出汗）、意识模糊 → **医疗紧急情况，立即拨打911**

**适应性（Acclimation）：**
- 需要1-2周的热暴露才能适应
- 季节初期的热浪最危险（身体尚未适应）
- 从凉爽地区旅行到炎热地区 → 暂时失去适应性

**最脆弱人群**：独居老人、幼儿、户外工作者、慢性病患者、没有空调的人

**安全要点**：多喝水、避免高温时段户外活动、绝不要把儿童或宠物留在车内、关注老年邻居。
